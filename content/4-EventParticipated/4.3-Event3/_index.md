### Main content
A study tour at AWS Vietnam where people working in the industry talked about the cloud job market and what the work is actually like, aimed at students. Five talks:

- **Mr. Nguyen Tran Minh Duy (Swinburne Vietnam)** opened by talking about the link between Swinburne and AWS and the spirit of the FCAJ community, where people who finish the program come back to help the ones behind them.
- **Mr. Nguyen Gia Hung (Head of Solution Architect, AWS Vietnam, founder of FCAJ)** gave the market picture. What I took from it: the number of jobs has not dropped across the whole industry, but hiring for junior roles has, so the bar for new graduates is much higher now because of AI. He suggested aiming at an industry (banking, retail, manufacturing) rather than just a role, and building visibility through community instead of waiting for job posts, since most positions are filled by referral.
- **Mr. Banh Cam Vinh (Data Engineer, Renova Cloud)** on the gap between school and real work: at school the data is clean and the requirements are fixed, but at work the data comes from many sources and the requirements keep changing. His advice that stuck with me was to learn the architecture, not just the tools, because tools change from company to company but the structure stays the same.
- **Ms. Nhu Tran (Account Manager, AWS Vietnam)** talked about fear and communication, more personal than technical. She shared that she applied to Amazon eleven times before getting in, and that most miscommunication is not because someone is wrong but because two people phrase the same thing differently.
- **Mr. Khang Nguyen (Solution Architect, Cloud Kinetics, Swinburne alumnus)** closed with skills and mindset for working in the AI era, speaking as someone who sat in the same classes not long ago.

### What I learned
This one was less about a product and more about the road ahead, so what I kept was mostly about direction. The part that stayed with me was Mr. Hung's point that the total amount of work has not shrunk, it has shifted toward senior roles, which means new graduates are pushed to grow faster and to use AI to solve problems that used to be done by hand. Mr. Vinh's idea of learning the architecture rather than the tools matches how I tried to build my own project, keeping the same structure even as the pieces changed. Ms. Nhu's story about applying eleven times was a useful reminder that persistence matters as much as skill.
